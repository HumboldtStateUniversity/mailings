The State of California finally has a budget for 2010-11, and the CSU system has recently provided details to the campuses. Based on that information, we now have a reasonably good estimate of our campus resources for the 2010-11 fiscal year.

The good news is that the CSU received a $260 million budget restoration for this year, in addition to $106 million in one-time federal stimulus funds. This was the first time the state increased funding for the CSU in three years. It brings state support to the level of five years ago, though we are serving more students.

For Humboldt State, the increase in state allocation and the stimulus funding combined amount to $7.4 million. That reflects an enrollment target increase for resident students that occurred earlier in this semester, from 6,612 to 6,814 full-time equivalent students (FTES). Our overall budget breaks down as follows:

  • State Appropriation $69,875,740
  • Fees/other revenue $37,405,199
  • Total $107,280,939

With fees and other revenues, this represents an increase of $12,028,589 over the prior year budget.

After mandatory costs, earmarked revenues and ARRA funds, the unallocated amount is $1,793,158. Below is a table that describes how the allocated funds will be used:

The state budget also called for additional funding if the CSU system reaches an enrollment level of 339,873 FTES. Because of that, Humboldt State University's resident enrollment target was recently adjusted to 7,010 FTES, which is just a bit above our enrollment in the prior year. If we are successful in meeting that target, we will receive an additional $2 million.

As you may recall from my previous message, most of the $2.5 million we received in federal stimulus (ARRA) funding is being used to offer additional class sections in the spring semester, while about $500,000 is being used for priorities in other areas. This focus on additional class sections is particularly important given the additional enrollment we are now expecting. Funds that are not used in the spring will be carried forward for use next fall.

The budget news, overall, is positive after the very difficult reductions of prior years. Our legislators and the governor should be applauded for investing in public higher education during very challenging times.

However, it is important that we remain cautious. California's economy is still struggling, with only limited signs that a modest recovery is underway. The newly passed state budget, by nearly all accounts, has left the next governor and legislature a deficit of billions of dollars. And next year, it is very unlikely that we will receive significant federal assistance as we have over the last two years. Our level of funding next year – and to an extent even for the remainder of this year – is difficult to predict.

Because of this, we will continue a conservative approach to managing our finances, even as we take advantage of opportunities to recover enrollment growth. We will also strive to continue efforts to be open and transparent in developing our budget. The University Budget Committee will be meeting throughout the fall and into next spring to consider the many factors that will impact our budget going forward. The committee will also discuss how our funds are being used, and they will consider a budget proposal put forward by the vice presidents and me.
