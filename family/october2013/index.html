Tips for Staying Professional Both In and Out of Class

According to a recent article in Bloomberg News, the number one thing that today’s college students lack is basic employability skills. The good news is that habits like punctuality and appropriate dress can be learned. We checked in with the HSU Career Center for some tips on how students can stay professional both in and out of class.

Arrive on time

“Arriving on time signals: I’m here and I’m ready to learn,” says Joy Soll, career counselor for the College of Professional Studies. Ideally, students are punctual because they’re excited about their classes, Soll says. But getting out of bed for that 8 a.m. macroeconomics class is a lot harder once November rolls around. “I like to remind students that professors are human too. They spend a lot of time preparing to make sure students get the most out of the 1–2 hours they’re in class,” Soll says. "Arriving on time is a sign of respect." Another thing to consider: professors frequently write letters of recommendation and serve as references for jobs and graduate school. Some employers even contact professors directly for names of potential job candidates. “Professors can become a valuable part of a student’s network,” Soll says.

Dress appropriately

For a lot of students, college is a time for experimenting: with dress, hair and other forms of personal expression. “I have a students who come in with piercings and tattoos and say â€˜I just wanna be me’,” Soll says. While there’s nothing wrong with being personally expressive, Soll reminds students that there’s a time and a place for everything. “If you’re going to drop off that resume, put on a button up shirt,” she says. And another thing: looking professional doesn’t have to break the bank. In fact, each February—as part of its annual Career Expo—the Career Center assembles an entire professional outfit from local second hand stores—demonstrating that students can look great, even when they’re on a budget.

Actively Participate

From not getting enough sleep to dealing with a looming deadline, there’s always an excuse for not being fully engaged at school or work. In rare instances, life gets in the way. If a student is faced with an emergency that requires checking their phone, Soll suggests telling their professor beforehand. In all other instances, turn your cell phone off or at least keep it on silent, she says. The exception, of course, is for classes where having Internet access—whether through a cell phone or other device—assists in conducting research.

In the same vein, body posture and eye contact go a long way in signaling engagement—not just in the classroom. Soll leads workshops on the importance of maintaining proper body language, especially in interviews. Hair twirling, finger tapping and other nervous ticks like constantly saying “umm” are no–nos. Another point to consider: norms regarding eye contact differ based on culture. “We live in a culture where eye contact is o.k.,” Soll says. But international students or those who come from immigrant families may have been raised to avert eye contact as a sign of deference. To feel as comfortable as possible, Soll suggests students stop by the Career Center for a mock interview. “That way, you can work out any kinks and feel 100 percent confident when you go out to get that job,” she says.

The Career Center is hosting a series of workshops on how to get an internship as part of Internship Week Oct. 21–24. Seminars include how to find internships overseas, in the sciences, humanities and business. For a full schedule, view the workshop pdf.

Enroll in the Affordable Care Act

Did you know that students have until March 31 to enroll in the Affordable Care Act, also known as 'Obamacare'? Under the new law, many CSU students will be eligible for either no-cost, public insurance under the newly expanded Medi-Cal program or heavily discounted (subsidized) private insurance purchased through a new government entity named Covered California. For more information, visit calstate.edu/coveredca.
