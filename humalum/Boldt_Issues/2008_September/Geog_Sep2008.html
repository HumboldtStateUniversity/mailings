HSU Students Set to Explore
Major Rivers of Asia


As part of the HSU China & Tibet Field Studies Program, 9 students will leave for the vast expanses of the Tibetan Plateau on September 8.

As part of its global outreach, the program has been offered by the HSU Geography Department since 2000 and over 80 students have taken advantage of this unusual opportunity to explore and document rarely seen parts of the world.

Tony Rossi, program director and Geography Department instructor, has designed this year’s travel plan to reflect the tremendous changes that are sweeping China and Tibet and a visit to post-Olympics Beijing will contrast sharply with what will be found in China’s vast hinterland.

Originally scheduled for summer, this year’s field trip will focus on central and far western Tibet, where the natural conditions will be particularity challenging. Arid and windy, the region contains traces of ancient trade routes and civilizations, surviving populations of Tibet’s endangered wildlife and numerous sacred sites. Principal among them is Mt. Kailash, one of the most venerated peaks in the world and focus of devout Hindu, Buddhist and Bon pilgrims.

Once underway, students will travel overland by 4 wheel drive vehicles and study a variety of subjects ranging from the impact of China’s rapid economic development to the current state of the regions fragile environment.

Past summers have taken classes to remote sections of the Yellow, Yangtze, Mekong and Salween Rivers and this fall the focus will be on the headwaters of the Indus, Ganges and Brahmaputtra Rivers. As the major river systems of South Asia, the quality and condition of their upstream water resources are of critical importance to hundreds of millions of people.

Whenever possible community based service projects are carried out such as helping renovate a rural clinic in eastern Tibet, contributing to the upkeep of a remote boarding school in northeastern Tibet and most recently returning to the same school and installing a student designed solar power system.

Students will return in late October and present their findings during Geographic Awareness Week (scheduled for mid-November)
