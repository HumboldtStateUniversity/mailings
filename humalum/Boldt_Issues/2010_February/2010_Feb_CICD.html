Redwood dug-out canoes, the American Indian Community and HSU

On February 6, 2010, the Center for Indian Community Development (CICD) hosted a well attended community sharing presentation to celebrate the completion of a traditional old growth redwood dug out canoe. Sponsored by the Me’Dil Institute, CICD and the Native Cultures Fund, the gathering featured a slide presentation and discussion on making the canoe from conception to completion. The presentation was followed by a Q&A period with the canoe makers, some of whom are HSU students, alumni and employees. The twenty foot long canoe was onsite for viewing. This event was an opportunity for the community to interact with experienced practicioners directly.

The Center for Indian Community Development, the Native Cultures Fund, the Blue Lake Rancheria and others supported Me’Dil’s 18-month endeavor to build this traditional old-growth redwood dugout canoe. The Me’Dil Institute is a small grassroots community organization dedicated to the continued expression of American Indian cultural practices and arts. The Me’Dil Institute and community members involved with making this canoe have also started on a second old-growth redwood dug out canoe.

Old growth redwood is becoming increasingly difficult to obtain and great care must be taken in transforming ancient redwood logs into traditional canoes. Me’Dil’s goals in creating these canoes included: (1) encouraging community involvement in traditional practices, (2) focusing on transmission of traditional knowledge, (3) incorporating modern tools and conveniences in an effort to make the practice more accessible in a contemporary setting and (4) documenting the process as an aid to practicioners as they further develop their own skill and in turn share knowledge with others in their families and communities.

Since 1966 the Center for Indian Community Development has provided services, opportunities and collaborative partnerships with American Indian communities in Northern California. Humboldt County has eight Indian Reservations lying within its borders. American Indian peoples make up over 6% of the population in Humboldt County, one of the highest concentrations of Native peoples in the state of California. Humboldt County is home to the state’s largest tribe (the Yurok Tribe), the state’s largest reservation (the Hoopa Valley Tribe) and also one of the smallest reservations in the entire United States (Blue Lake Rancheria).

Humboldt State University is home to a number of acclaimed American Indian focused programs which include CICD, the Indian Teacher Education Personnel Program (ITEPP) and the Indian Natural Resources, Sciences and Engineering Program (INRSEP). These programs not only provide educational support and a sense of community on the Humboldt State campus, but also provide real opportunities for collaboration, partnership and outreach in the surrounding community.

For the past forty years CICD has designed and implemented innovative projects that bring together the American Indian community with Humboldt State University. CICD collaborates with community members, Tribes, Indian organizations, educational institutions, students and government representatives on hundreds of projects. CICD also dedicates staff, materials, and resources to support projects, programs and goals. CICD is one of the primary ways that Humboldt State shows its dedication to the community they are a part of while also continuing to provide opportunities for students, faculty and alumni to gain access to the American Indian community in a culturally sensitive and appropriate way.
