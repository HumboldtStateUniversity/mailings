Carol Diebel—A Global Passion

Feb. 15, 2010 — Dorothyann Guido / Student Writer

Adventure is one thing Carol Diebel has not shied away from in her science career. Diebel was recently hired as the new director of the University of Alaska Museum of the North, the only research and teaching museum in Alaska. Before taking on the new post, Diebel served as the director of natural environment at the Museum of New Zealand Te Papa Tongarewa, where she led the museum's natural history research, curatorial and collections teams.

Diebel (’74) had been living in New Zealand for over 10 years. She moved there to do postdoctoral research in magnetic sense—a professional risk, since her background was in marine biology. Moving to New Zealand was also a personal risk, since it would take her far from her home in Bakersfield, California and connections in the U.S.

After four years working on a postdoc, Diebel took a curator position at a museum in Auckland. She says, “It was very difficult to make the move out of research and I actually felt a real loss of identity as a serious scientist at first. However, I have never regretted the move. This job allowed me to finally work on projects that would communicate science to general audiences.”

Moving from New Zealand to Alaska has been its own adventure. In addition to her new museum director post, she will serve a joint appointment with the University of Alaska Fairbank's School of Fisheries and Ocean Sciences as professor of marine biology. “This is another wonderful opportunity for me. The Museum of the North is affiliated with a university, has a dynamic staff and mix of collections (natural history, ethnology, and art) that fits well with my expertise.”

In addition to her Bachelor of Science degree from HSU, Diebel holds a doctorate in biological oceanography from the joint program in oceanography at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology-Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution. She transferred to Humboldt State in 1973, living in a mobile home with five roommates while exploring the diversity of science classes at HSU. “I decided to be a biology major so that I could take botany, biology, zoology, and even oceanography courses. I have always had trouble focusing on a single subject and still consider myself more of a generalist than a specialist.” She laughs as she recalls, “I even met my first boyfriend standing in line for a biology class.”

When asked what advice she could give to current and future students at HSU, she said, “Don’t end up working in an area that you don’t have a passion for. It will be difficult to sustain your motivation. And don’t forget—you can learn a lot more from failures than from successes in life.”
