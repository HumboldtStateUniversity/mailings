Major Changes for the 41st Humboldt Film Festival

This March 24-29, Humboldt County will be home to a "new and improved"Humboldt Film Festival. The festival, which has the distinction of being the oldest student-run film festival in the world, is now in its forty-first year -- a year which brought some major changes with it. Festival organizers explain that all of these changes were made in an effort to make the festival more accessible to the entire Humboldt community. They include a reduced general admission price, free access to all events for anyone with a current student ID (this includes College of the Redwoods students and county high school students), and a larger, more accessible venue in the form of the Van Duzer Theatre. Other changes include an increased internet presence for the festival, which has nearly quadrupled the number of submissions, resulting in a more diverse pool of films from all over the world.

The decision to make the festival free to students and reduce general admission prices was envisioned by Rick Hedstrom, one of the three student co-directors of this year's fest. This will be the first time the festival has reached out to students in this way, and the festival organizers hope this translates into more young movie fans being able to participate in and be inspired by this Humboldt tradition. The general admission prices will be reduced to $5 per day for the rest of the community, which includes screenings at 3 p.m. by judges with national reputations, as well as the 7 p.m. competitive screenings. There will also be the option to buy a week-long pass at the discounted price of $15, which includes all festival events. The 1 p.m. judges' workshops will be free to all, as in previous years.

The venue for this year's festival screenings is a change from recent years in which it was held at the Minor Theatre. Although this change may come as a surprise to some in the community, the festival's co-directors stress that this is actually a return to the fest's original home on the HSU campus at its 1967 inception. This would seem to be a good year for the festival to come home, as The Van Duzer recently acquired a state-of-the-art projector that is capable of producing a much larger, crisper image than the digital projectors used in recent years. As the festival organizers point out, the Van Duzer theatre also has a better sound system, more comfortable seating, and plenty of  parking spaces. Even the concessions stand at intermission is getting an upgrade: the festival plans to go "beyond popcorn," adding fresh local coffee, pastries, and even pizza to the mix.

This year one of the organizers' main priorities was "bringing the festival into the twenty-first century." They began using the services of withoutabox.com, a website that allows filmmakers from all over the world to search for and enter festivals that meet certain criteria. This is a service they had used before in the past, when it was a small start-up, but now, as festival co-director Aimee Hennessy points out, "like so much of what's on the internet today, it's gone from novelty to necessity." Filmmakers now expect to be able to access and pay for everything online. What this modernization brings to the Humboldt Film Festival is a more diverse mix of submissions. So far, the festival has received films from over twenty U.S. states and over a dozen countries.

"This monumental number of submissions provides a greater diversity of
topics, genres, styles, and filmmaker origins," points out Ben Bettenhausen, HSU grad student and festival co-director. "The overall quality of these submissions has improved, allowing us to present a more enjoyable festival program for all kinds of film viewer."

This year the Humboldt Film Festival will be arranged into Local Filmmakers Night, Experimental & Animation Day,  Documentary Day, Narrative Day, and Best of the Fest. The goal is to provide something for every taste, but the festival co-directors encourage viewers to attend each day to broaden their appreciation of film in general.

Local Filmmakers Night will kick off the festival on Monday, March 24th at 7 p.m.. It will be a showcase of the best in contemporary Humboldt County filmmaking. This will include HSU student films, HSU alumni films, and films from community members.

Experimental and Animation Day (Wednesday the 26th), will be judged by New York filmmaker Ry Russo-Young. She will be showing her short film 'Marion' and giving a free workshop on experimental filmmaking at 1 p.m.. At 3 p.m., she will be screening her new feature film 'Orphans,' which won a special jury prize at the 2007 SXSW Film Festival and continues to screen at numerous festivals in the U.S. and abroad. Orphans is the story of two estranged sisters who reunite at an isolated farmhouse and relive their dark past. At 7 p.m., Russo-Young will judge the competitive international festival screenings in the Experimental and Animation categories.

Documentary Day (Thursday the 27th) will be judged by Joaquin Alvarado. Alvarado is   an award-winning Chicano filmmaker whose works include The Silent Cross. He is also the Founding Director of the Institute for Next Generation Internet at SFSU, President of the Board for the Bay Area Video Coalition, and a leading member of the City of San Francisco Digital Media Advisory Committee. He will be giving a free workshop at 1 p.m., a multimedia presentation at 3 p.m., and judging the competitive international Documentary screenings at 7 p.m.

Narrative Day (Friday the 28th) will be presided over by Lori Petty. Petty is a well-known actress, as well as a director, a writer, a painter, and a clothing designer. She has played alongside Madonna and Geena Davis in A League of Their Own, opposite Keanu Reeves in Point Break, opposite Robin Williams in Cadillac Man, and alongside Gina Gershon in Prey For Rock 'n Roll. Petty also played the title character in the post-apocalyptic cult classic Tank Girl, based on the comic of the same name. She will be giving a free workshop at 1 p.m., showing one of her films (TBA) at 3 p.m., and judging the competitive international Narrative screenings at 7 p.m.

Best of the Fest Night (Saturday the 29th) will be emceed by Lori Petty. The winners in the Experimental, Animation, Documentary, and Narrative categories will be screened again, along with the Audience Choice Award winner. This event starts at 7 p.m.

All events are scheduled to take place in the Van Duzer Theatre, which is located on the HSU campus.Tickets for the Humboldt Film Festival will be available at the door during festival week events. For more information, go to www.humboldt.com/filmfest. The festival co-directors can be contacted at filmfest@humboldt.edu, or 707-826-4113.
